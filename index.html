Shifting Competencies: Strategy vs. Execution

As you move from Global to Local, the required skillset shifts dramatically. Corporate teams require high business acumen and systems thinking, while local teams require deep technical safety knowledge and operational influence.

Global Profile

Focus on data analysis, change management, executive influence, and standardizing processes across diverse cultures.

Local Profile

Focus on hazard recognition, regulatory compliance (OSHA/HSE), employee training, and emergency response.

Every EHS structure must solve the conflict between maintaining Control & Consistency (Centralized) and fostering Customization & Agility (Decentralized). The Hybrid/Matrix model (recommended) is the synthesis designed to achieve the best of both worlds.[8]

Feedback From HR EU + MEA December 2025 Meeting

Topic: How can we drive Safe Behaviours in our workplace? The following points highlight necessary interventions across psychological safety, leadership accountability, training, and systemic support.[35]

1. The Psychosocial Dimension (Mental Health = Safety)

  • Broaden the Definition of "Injury": Move beyond Lost Time Injuries (LTI) caused by accidents. We must account for long-term sick leave due to stress, which is often a precursor to physical lapses in safety.
  • The "Early Support Model" (Finland Example): Adopt a proactive intervention strategy. Supporting employees at the first signs of stress or illness prevents the exhaustion that leads to accidents.[36]
  • Addressing Time Pressure: Investigate "pressure to fix" as a root cause. High time pressure creates a cognitive load that causes employees to bypass safety protocols.

2. Leadership & Accountability (The "Top-Down" Mirror)

  • Operational Blindness: Managers must conduct weekly "Safety Walks" and "Safety Talks" to see the workplace with fresh eyes.
  • Safety as a Meeting Opener: Standardize the "Safety Moment" at the start of every meeting (e.g., discussing the commute to work or a recent near-miss).
  • Leading by Example: Managers must model behavior in common areas, storage rooms, and offices‚Äînot just on the shop floor. This includes maintaining "Order and Cleanliness" as a fundamental safety habit.

3. Behavioral Reinforcement & Learning (The "Habit" Loop)

  • Scenario-Based Learning: Replace generic slides with real-life role plays and VR simulations. Experiencing the consequences of an unsafe act in a safe environment creates a stronger "somatic marker" (emotional memory).
  • No-Blame Reporting: Use tools like Quentic to encourage "Psychological Safety." Employees must feel safe to report near-misses without fear of punishment.
  • Public Recognition: Transition from "policing" to "praising." Publicly recognize and reward safe behaviors to reinforce them as the social norm.

4. Systemic Support & Governance (The "Infrastructure")

  • Standardized Local Roles: Audit the H&S responsible roles in each country to ensure consistency in resources, authority, and time allocation.
  • Resource Allocation: Ensure the "Service Team" has the actual tools, equipment, and personnel needed to work safely. Safety-first is impossible if the team is under-resourced.
  • Bottom-Up Feedback Loop: Regularly ask: "We have the tools and training‚Äîwhat is still missing?" Use these insights to review and simplify work instructions.
